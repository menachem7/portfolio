Workspace

Rethinking cloud storage to rival Google Drive and iCloud

Client

Bloc

Role

UX/UI Design

Date

2018

Intro

During my time at Bloc I was given the challenge of designing a cloud file storage solution that could rival current competitors. Competitors seemed to have every possible solution but utilizing user research I found an opportunity. Stealing a card from Steve Jobs, I tried to follow the “less is more” principle throughout each phase of the product cycle. I was responsible for all UX / UI and delivered a Desktop Prototype with all the necessary branding.

Problem

A market dominated by tech giants such as Google and Apple with seemingly little to no room left for innovation.

Solution

Listening to users. Users indicated that a new service offering improved file organization could win them over.

Discovery

Project Brief

“Become the next big player in the cloud storage market."

Product must have the ability to:

1

Store, organize and create files

2

Share and work on files with others

3

Choose privacy settings for files

4

Be accessible via desktop and mobile

The product capabilities set for this challenge reflected what most cloud file storage platforms offered. In order to “become the next big player” I would need to find a way to differentiate the product.

Competitive Analysis & User Research

I began with a competitive analysis, finding Google Drive, iCloud, Dropbox and Box to be the biggest players in the cloud file storage industry, with Google clearly leading the pack. I performed a SWOT Analysis to better understand each service’s hold on the market and created user flows to understand their products. Room for innovation seemed bleak.

❮ ❯

I then conducted user surveys on over 20 participants to understand user perspectives and behaviors. Users proved my SWOT analysis correct, as over 80% of users prefered Google Drive to any other service. In trying to find an opportunity, users became my North Star, and indicated that organizing files was their greatest pain point.

“I don't like that Google Drive doesn't allow you to manually order items and that you have to choose one of their order methods (alphabetical, last modified, etc)”

“Just overall organization is not very intuitive”
Strategy

User Personas

Cloud storage users indicating a need for improved file organization fell into two main demographics: 15 - 25, predominantly male (80%) and 25-35, predominantly female (60%). I targeted two individuals who aligned with the demographics of my target market and created user personas to guide me through the strategy phase.

❮ ❯

User Stories

With my users in mind I began creating user stories, including all user tasks whether painstakingly obvious or seemingly unnecessary. Using the Pareto Principle, I segmented 20% of the user stories as High Priority and 80% as Medium or Low Priority, preventing feature bloat and ensuring I could deliver an MVP a.s.a.p.

Innovation

During this phase I took the opportunity to try and solve the users’ pain point, hoping to innovate a way in which to make file organization easier.

My Goals

1

Make file organization easier

2

Remove clutter.

3

Give users ability to create more order.

Smart Organization

An account option requiring users to create personalized folder structures and follow a template when uploading and creating files to ensure they are named, tagged and placed into folders right away.

Unsorted Files

A section for files that are unnamed and lacking a folder, while the main dashboard only displays named files placed into folders.

User Flows and Site Map

Innovation is great but not when it leads to a worse experience. Inspired by Dropbox’s onboarding process which gives users the option of personalizing their account type before they sign onto the platform for the first time, I designed the Smart Organization feature to be setup during the onboarding process.

Design

Wireframes and Usability Test

I began sketching the site and once I had the basic outline moved into Figma to create digital wireframes and Invision for usability testing. Users found the design simple and clean, but the functionality to be confusing. The wireframe was missing many crucial details and the Smart Organization feature seemed more annoying than helpful. I decided to abandon the Smart Organization feature and returned to the “whiteboard” hoping to create a more intuitive wireframe.

Branding

Simply put, users wanted a way to organize their files better. But what was the underlying desire of wanting to be organized? Does it generate a feeling, inspire a mindset, let them breathe? And if I could pinpoint what it was, could I encapsulate and communicate those abstract elements into something concrete, into... a brand?

Hi-Fi Mockup and Preference Tests

With a revamped wireframe and clean brand guide in hand, I created the first high-fidelity mockup. To abstain from subjective bias I redesigned a few screens and sent them to users for preference testing.

“Uploading is an important and frequently used button that got lost in the other design; it is easy to find here.”
“I think it puts the importance of this sharing feature front and center, the other feels I can just push it to the side.”

Final Prototype

I implemented the preferred screens and had the final prototype go through a final round of usability testing. To my delight, users loved the branding, remarking it was “welcoming and peaceful” and navigated through the app with ease. An additional surprise came when users noticed the Unsorted Files feature (which went unnoticed during the wireframe testing). Ecstatic with the feature, many complained that their current file dashboard was a chaotic mess of named and unnamed files that made tracking down a file a nightmare.

1 - 0, Workspace!

Conclusion

This project was challenging and had me scratching my head quite a few times but at the finish line I had delivered a product that fulfilled the client’s brief and left users nodding their heads. Along the way my flaws were definitely exposed: an underdeveloped a wireframe, an organization feature to hastily launched. My excited, yet impatient drive to enact my vision overshadowing the real needs of users. I take pride in the final product but embrace my failures. I learned that a good product requires patience during each step of the process and that a little bit more time spent during the planning stage can save you a lot of time (and money) during the execution stage.
